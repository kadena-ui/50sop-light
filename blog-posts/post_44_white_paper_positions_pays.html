Why a white paper
is not what most
people think it is
Most consultants think of white papers as something large corporations publish to justify their existence.
The right kind of white paper is none of those things.
It is a concise, authoritative document that takes a specific position on a pressing industry problem — and makes the case for your approach as the most intelligent solution.
When a prospective client reads it, they do not think "interesting research."
They think: "This is exactly how I need to be thinking. Who wrote this? I need to talk to them."

What separates a
white paper that sells
from one that sits
  • A contrarian position — the most effective white papers challenge a widely held belief. Agreement does not generate leads; disruption does.
  • A specific audience — a white paper for everyone reaches no one. The narrower the audience, the more essential it feels to them.
  • Original data or evidence — even 50 survey responses creates proprietary evidence no one else can replicate or cite without referencing you
  • A named framework — your approach must have a name. Unnamed methodology cannot be remembered, referenced, or licensed.
  • A clear next step at the end — every reader who finishes it should know exactly what you offer and how to engage you
