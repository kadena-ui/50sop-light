Why most offsites
fail — and what
the right design does
Most executive offsites are built around a slideshow. Someone presents. The team listens. There is a dinner. Everyone flies home with a list of actions that evaporates within a week.
The problem is not commitment. It is design. An offsite built around facilitated decision-making — where the right conversations happen in the right sequence, decisions are made publicly, and outputs are documented in real time — produces alignment that a year of weekly meetings cannot replicate.
The consultant who designs and facilitates this experience is not running a meeting. They are producing a strategic outcome that the organisation will reference for months.

How a high-impact
offsite is structured
Executive Offsite · Design Framework
Pre-offsite
Discovery and agenda design
Interviews with senior leaders, diagnosis of the core tensions, and design of the session sequence before anyone arrives
Day 1 AM
State of the business and honest assessment
Facilitated truth-telling — what is working, what is not, and what everyone knows but no one has said publicly
Day 1 PM
Strategic choices and decision-making
The highest-stakes decisions made publicly, with accountability assigned before the day ends
Day 2
Execution planning and alignment
Priorities, owners, 90-day actions, and the communication plan for taking decisions back to the organisation
Post-offsite
Documented output and follow-up
A written summary of decisions, priorities, and commitments — delivered within 48 hours

4
How to position this
so leadership teams
say yes immediately
  • Lead with the cost of a bad offsite — a leadership team of 12 people spending two days away from the business represents $100,000 or more in combined compensation and opportunity cost. A poorly designed offsite wastes every dollar of it.
  • Separate design from facilitation in the proposal — most companies do not realise they are paying for both. When you price them separately, you demonstrate the depth of what the work actually involves.
  • Target the CEO and COO directly — the executive assistant often schedules the offsite but the CEO owns the outcome. Speak to the person who will be held accountable for whether it produced results.
  • Propose the annual retainer after the first offsite — a leadership team that experienced a genuinely transformative offsite will book you for the next one before they have left the building
  • Deliver the documented output within 48 hours — the written output is what transforms a good experience into a business investment. It is also your most powerful referral tool.
