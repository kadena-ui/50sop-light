Why revenue share is
the highest-trust model
in consulting
A client who pays a fee is buying your time. A client who shares revenue with you is betting on your expertise. The distinction is enormous — because one relationship ends when the invoice is paid, and the other continues as long as the results compound.
Revenue share is not appropriate for every engagement. It is appropriate for engagements where the outcome is measurable, the timeline is defined, and the value you create significantly exceeds any fee you could justify charging up front.
When the client grows revenue by $2 million because of your work, a 10% share pays you $200,000. No retainer conversation would have reached that number. And the client paid nothing until the result existed.

How to structure and
protect a revenue
share deal
  • Establish the baseline before you start — every revenue share deal requires a documented starting point. Without an agreed baseline, the percentage calculation is disputed. Baseline first, always.
  • Define the measurement method in writing — how will results be measured, who provides the data, and how often will reporting occur. Ambiguity here destroys trust and ends partnerships.
  • Cap the term, not the upside — revenue share agreements should have a defined term (12 to 36 months). Cap the duration. Never cap the percentage — that removes your incentive to over-deliver.
  • Use a hybrid model for new clients — a reduced fee plus a performance component is the lowest-risk entry point for both parties and the fastest path to building the trust that a pure share arrangement requires
  • Only take share deals where you control the variables — a revenue share only works when you have meaningful influence over the outcome. Never agree to a share deal where success depends on client actions you cannot affect.
