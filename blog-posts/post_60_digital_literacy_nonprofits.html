Why nonprofits are
chronically behind —
and why that matters
The average nonprofit operates on technology that is 5 to 10 years behind the private sector. Not because the staff are incapable — because the funding for digital capacity has been deprioritised by funders who want to see programme outcomes, not infrastructure investment.
The result: organisations with powerful missions are losing donors to better-designed emails, losing volunteers to harder-to-navigate websites, and losing community members to platforms they do not know how to use.
The digital literacy gap is a mission delivery problem. And the consultant who solves it gets paid by the organisation — and often by the funder.

How to get nonprofits
to fund training they
think they cannot afford
  • Write it into the grant budget — capacity building is a fundable line item in most foundation grants. Help clients include training in their next proposal.
  • Approach funders directly — foundations that fund nonprofits in your niche will often fund digital capacity work separately. Pitch them directly.
  • Offer a multi-org cohort — five organisations sharing one training cost makes premium consulting accessible and increases your per-session revenue
  • Frame it as mission protection — a nonprofit that cannot communicate its impact digitally loses donors and credibility. Frame digital literacy as essential, not optional.
  • Start with a free diagnostic — a short digital audit reveals gaps that create urgency for the full engagement
